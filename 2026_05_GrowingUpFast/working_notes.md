# Working Notes

## Core Theme

This is a story about fatherhood as a long act of loving someone enough to let them move away from you. It begins with absolute protection: a father holding an infant with a fierce, almost animal certainty that he would die before letting harm reach him. It moves into the golden years of childhood, when the son wants to be near him, copy him, learn from him, and orbit him. In that period the father is not just loved, but central. He is the measure of safety, knowledge, strength, and wonder.

Then comes adolescence, when that same love has to change shape. The boy becomes withdrawn, sharp-edged, unimpressed, and restless. He turns away from the father, not because the bond was false, but because independence demands distance. The father has to endure the ache of no longer being the centre of his son's world, and has to understand that this apparent rejection is part of the making of a man.

In the final movement, the son stands fully grown. The father looks at him with pride, but that pride is mixed with grief for the vanished child who once reached for his hand and looked at him as if he hung the stars. The father is no longer the sun in the son's sky, but that is not failure. It is fulfillment. The little boy is gone, but in his place is a powerful man the father helped shape, a man now strong enough to comfort the father in return.

## Free Writing

When he was small, love felt simple because it had a direction. It ran outward from me like heat. I did not have to think about what I would give up for him because the answer was everything. He arrived fragile and complete, and something ancient woke in me. I wanted to stand in every doorway before he reached it. I wanted to bargain with time, with fate, with God if necessary. I wanted his pain to stop at me.

At first every small achievement feels like a miracle announced to the room. A first word. A first step. The first time he manages something by himself and does not yet know it is ordinary, so you are allowed to treat it as sacred. Parenthood in those years is partly exhaustion and partly pageantry. You cheer for everything. You keep score of wonder. You build your life around these tiny proofs that the child is arriving more fully into the world.

Then came the years I did not know, while I was living them, would one day feel holy in memory. The years of small shoes by the door, of questions without end, of a face tipping upward to see whether I was watching. The years when my habits became his lessons. The way I tied something, fixed something, laughed at something, all of it was gathered up by him as if I were showing him how to be in the world. There is no applause for that season while you are in it, but later you realise you were being trusted in the deepest possible way.

There is also the soft foolishness of wanting to spoil him, not from guilt or weakness but from delight. Wanting to come home with some toy, some treat, some small surprise, just to watch happiness light up his whole body. There is a kind of wealth in being able to create that joy. Not luxury, exactly, but abundance measured in his face. A father learns that sometimes giving is less about the object than about the privilege of witnessing joy arrive.

As he grows, pride changes again. It is no longer only about dependence or closeness, but competence. You watch him sit exams, walk into tests you cannot take for him, perform on a stage or field where all you can do is stand back and witness. The child who once needed your hand begins proving he has hands of his own. That pride is cleaner and lonelier. It asks less of you, but it costs more.

And then, almost without announcement, the weather changes. The child who once ran toward you begins to close doors. He speaks less. He tests everything. He is embarrassed by tenderness, impatient with advice, resistant to whatever looks inherited. You can mistake it for contempt if you are vain, or for failure if you are frightened. But really it is construction. He is tearing down the shrine because he cannot become himself while kneeling at it.

This is also the age of performance in another sense: the first girlfriend, the first experiments in adulthood, the first bad imitation of ease. Half a first beer swallowed with a brave face, pretending to like the bitterness because liking it looks like manhood. These moments are funny and aching at once. You can see the boy still there inside the posture, but you also know the posture matters. He is trying on distance, trying on coolness, trying on a self that is separate from yours.

That may be the hardest lesson for the father: not how to protect, not how to teach, but how to step back without making the withdrawal about his own wound. To understand that love is no less real when it stops looking grateful. To know that being needed is not the same as being important. To accept that the boy must leave your shadow, even if you miss the warmth of having him there.

When I look at the man he becomes, I feel both enlargement and loss. I miss the boy who thought I knew everything. I miss the hand that fit inside mine. I miss being the answer to his fear. But the world now has him in full size, and I can see what all that distance was for. Strength. Judgment. Independence. Kindness that has survived friction. And if one day his hand steadies me, if his voice calms mine, that is not the story turning cruel. That is the story coming true.

Adulthood announces itself in quieter symbols than childhood did. Not first steps now, but moving out. Coming back to visit by choice. Earning money. Turning up with a bottle of wine under his arm as if to say he is no longer only receiving hospitality but bringing it. Then, later still, helping me lift something, sort something, mend something, endure something. The old exchange reverses itself so slowly you barely notice until it has happened: the one I carried now carries some part of me.

## Emotional Centre

The emotional centre is the father's double truth: he is proud that his son has grown beyond him, and heartbroken that growth required the disappearance of the child who adored him.

## Characters or Point of View

Primary voice: the father, speaking with hindsight.

Secondary presence: the son at different ages, almost as if he is several versions of the same person seen across time.

Possible approach: keep the son mostly observed rather than explained, so the emotion stays grounded in what the father sees, remembers, and loses.

## Setting and World

The setting can stay mostly domestic and ordinary. That helps the emotional arc feel universal rather than overly staged.

Useful world details:

- The house as a map of growth: nursery, hallway, garden, doorway, bedroom door closed.
- Shared routines: making things, fixing things, walking somewhere, sitting in the car, watching, teaching.
- Passage of time through objects: toys, tools, shoes, coats, height marks, keys.
- Markers of age and independence: exam papers, sports kit or performance clothes, a first beer, house keys, a bottle of wine brought through the door.

## Conflict or Tension

The main conflict is internal. The father must learn that the son's distance is not betrayal, and that successful parenting often feels, in the middle act, like abandonment. He must grieve without clinging, and stay present without demanding to be central.

## Questions to Explore

- What exact moments made the father realise he was no longer the son's sun?
- Which firsts matter most in the memory: first word, first step, first exam, first love, first drink, first home of his own?
- What did the father teach deliberately, and what did the son learn simply by watching?
- What did the father get wrong during the teenage years?
- What part of the son's growing up felt like loss, and what part felt like reward?
- Which symbol best shows adulthood arriving: the girlfriend, the beer, moving out, or the bottle of wine?
- What does it mean for the son to comfort the father now?
- Is the final feeling more acceptance, gratitude, grief, or all three at once?

## Song Structure Ideas

6-verse narrative map (recommended):

- Verse 1 (Father): birth and infant ferocity; protection and awe
- Verse 2 (Father): first words, first steps, toys, treats, father as centre
- Verse 3 (Son): teenage distance and first attempts at manhood (girlfriend, beer, defiance)
- Verse 4 (Father): pride at competence (exams, performance), mixed with felt loss
- Verse 5 (Son): adult return (moving out, visiting, wine, helping his father)
- Verse 6 (Wife to father's gravestone): final word; she is comforted by the man her son became, and names both losses and gains

Voice order across song: Father -> Father -> Son -> Father -> Son -> Wife

Ending intention: the emotional landing should be the wife finding comfort in the son as living proof of the father's love and legacy.

Current structure decision: no full chorus.

Use repeating verse architecture instead:

- Start father and wife verses with: Do you remember, darling dear?
- Start son verses with: Do you remember, mother dear?
- End each father verse with: I miss our boy, but hail the man.
- End each son verse with: I was that boy, became this man.
- End wife final verse with one legacy line (choose one and keep it fixed)
- Optional: use a one-line refrain between selected verses only if needed for lift

## Style Guidance

## Stylistic Influence Notes

Influence reference only. Use these for craft direction, not for borrowing lines, hooks, melody contours, or verse shapes.

From Bob Dylan's The Times They Are A-Changin':

- Plain, direct language that sounds spoken rather than ornamental
- Strong rhetorical framing and repeated address lines that build momentum
- Verses that escalate by adding example after example
- Broad life truths delivered in simple, memorable phrasing

How that applies here:

- Keep "Do you remember..." as a recurring rhetorical engine
- Let each verse move one step forward in time
- Favor clarity and inevitability over clever wording

From Harry Chapin's Cat's in the Cradle:

- Narrative time jumps across life stages with clear scene markers
- Emotional impact built through repetition and delayed realization
- Parent-child role reversal as a central dramatic turn
- Domestic details used as emotional anchors

How that applies here:

- Track growth through specific moments: first steps, exams, first beer, moving out, returning with wine
- Keep repeating end-lines by voice so meaning deepens with each age
- Build toward the final reversal where the son steadies the parent

Originality guardrails:

- Do not imitate signature phrases, rhyme schemes, or melodic patterns from reference songs
- Keep this lyric anchored in your own symbols: sun, orbit, shelter, tools, doorway, gravestone, wine bottle
- Keep the final emotional move unique: wife gets the last word, comforted by the man her son became

Reference point, not template: aim for the plain-spoken, folk-storytelling clarity found in well-known narrative songs, but do not borrow their phrases, rhyme patterns, melody shapes, or exact verse architecture.

Useful traits from that tradition:

- Simple language that sounds spoken rather than literary
- Verses that move the story forward one image or incident at a time
- Repetition used for weight and memory rather than cleverness
- Moral force coming from observation, not from preaching too hard
- Strong emotional pull created by ordinary details rather than ornate poetry
- A tune and chord feel that can carry many words cleanly

What is useful from a Dylan-like approach in this context:

- Direct, uncluttered lines that feel like someone standing up and telling the truth
- Broad social or life meaning carried by very plain wording
- Verses that accumulate power by stacking examples rather than over-explaining
- A chorus or refrain that feels proverbial, like a truth the singer had to learn slowly

What is useful from a narrative family-song approach in this context:

- Clear passage of time from verse to verse
- Recurring line or chorus that changes meaning as the son grows older
- Specific life markers doing the heavy lifting: first steps, exams, first beer, moving out, bottle of wine, steadying his father
- Emotional restraint; the sadness lands harder when the song does not beg for tears

How to keep this original:

- Use your own symbols: sun, orbit, shelter, tools, doorways, bottle of wine
- Keep the emotional argument centered on successful fatherhood feeling like a loss in the middle and a fulfillment at the end
- Let the son's growth be the plot, rather than building the song around regret alone
- Avoid any borrowed catchphrase structure from famous songs; write a refrain built from this father-son cosmology instead

Verse guidance:

- Default meter target: 8/8/8/8 across each verse (8 syllables per line)
- Use "Do you remember, darling dear?" as the cadence anchor for counting and phrasing
- Allow occasional +/-1 syllable only if the sung phrasing still lands naturally
- Default rhyme strategy: ABCB (ballad form) for most verses
- Secondary options when needed: AABB or loose/free rhyme
- Favor slant rhymes frequently over perfect rhymes
- Sentiment first: never force a rhyme if it distorts meaning, voice, or emotional truth
- Each verse should contain 2 or 3 concrete moments, not a whole era in abstract
- End verses on an image that can lean into the chorus
- Let the details age with the son: cot, shoes, toys, exam hall, beer glass, keys, wine bottle, steady hands
- Keep the father in scene as observer, teacher, or remembered centre of gravity

Refrain guidance (no full chorus):

- Keep the song verse-led, with narrative momentum through time
- If a refrain is used, keep it to one short line and reuse sparingly
- Preferred refrain language should support the central paradox without resetting the story
- Candidate refrain line: From the child you bore for us, to the man we gave the world

Wider style notes:

- Folky story song, moderate tempo, room for many words
- Acoustic-first feel; the lyric should work with just voice and guitar
- Rhyme should support speech, not dominate it; clarity and truth matter more than tidy rhyme
- Prefer honest phrasing over clever turns of phrase
- If a line sounds too composed, simplify it until it sounds lived-in
- Start in warmth, laughter, and delight; let the ache arrive later
- The ending tone should feel resigned and loving rather than bitter
- Final image should carry comfort, not collapse: the wife is not alone, because the son now steadies her

Possible refrain directions:

- Once I was your sun, now I watch your fire
- You moved out of my orbit, not out of my love
- I miss the boy, but I bless the man
- I was your shelter then, you are my strength now

Voice idea to explore:

- Father addresses the mother in a private monologue: "do you remember when..."
- The son can be addressed more indirectly, or possibly get a brief answering line to his mother in a later bridge
- This keeps the lyric reflective and intimate, and avoids sounding like a lecture from father to son
- It also lets both parents share ownership of the pride and the loss: "the man we gave the world"

Voice tag rule for verse endings:

- Father and wife verses start with: Do you remember, darling dear?
- Son verses start with: Do you remember, mother dear?
- Any verse in the father's voice ends with: I miss our boy, but hail the man.
- Any verse in the son's voice ends with: I was that boy, became this man.
- Wife final verse can end with either of these options:
- I miss my love, but hail our men.
- From the child you held so close, to the man we gave the world.
- Keep wife's final line grounded in comfort from the son, not only grief for the father.
- End each verse with one closing tag line only
- Keep the chorus untagged so both voices can share it

**The Man We Gave the World**
*Working Draft*

---

**Verse 1 — Father**

Do you remember, darling dear,
the night I drove him safely home?
I held him like the world might break —
my heart was no longer my own.
He slept so small inside my arms,
one breath, one heartbeat, one soft sound —
I would have moved the earth itself
to keep that little life from harm.
*I miss our boy, but hail the man.*

---

**Verse 2 — Father**

Do you remember, darling dear,
his first word ringing down the hall?
His first steps crossed the kitchen floor
and I stood ready for his fall.
I'd come home with some little thing —
a toy, a treat, some small surprise —
just for the joy that filled the room
when happiness lit up his eyes.
*I miss our boy, but hail the man.*

---

**Verse 3 — Son**

Do you remember, mother dear,
the way I followed him around?
I watched him tie, and fix, and build —
in his orbit I was bound.
He was the measure of all things,
the strongest man I'd ever known —
safe within his shadow, learned
what I'd need for life alone.
*I was that boy, became this man.*

---

**Verse 4 — Son**

Do you remember, mother dear,
the years I hid behind my door?
No smiles would fill the house with joy,
no human shadow anymore.
A voice that answered just in grunts,
a spotty face you'd rarely see —
was learning how to build the man
I was always meant to be.
*I was that boy, became this man.*

---

**Verse 5 — Father**

Do you remember, darling dear,
the day I watched him standing tall?
He didn't take my offered hand —
and didn't stumble, didn't fall.
We stood like brothers in a photo,
and he would have thanked me in his speech —
so many names upon the page,
there wasn't time for him to reach.
*I miss our boy, but hail the man.*

---

**Verse 6 — Son**

Do you remember, mother dear,
the first time I returned home?
My graduation photo proud
beside a silent telephone.
Found dusty tools upon the shelf —
I took the weight he used to lift,
I steadied father's hands so he could
tie, and build, and fix.
*I was that boy, became this man.*

---

**Verse 7 — Wife**

Do you remember, my dear love,
the day we laid you in the ground?
I thought I couldn't carry on —
we'd fall like trees without a sound.
But then our son reached out his hand,
the same sure hands you taught to build.
I felt you there inside his grip,
the promise quietly fulfilled.
*I miss you dear, but hail the man.*

---

*Voice order: Father · Father · Son · Son · Father · Son · Wife*
